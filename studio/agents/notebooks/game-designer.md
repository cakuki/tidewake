### 2026-07-01 — Deep-learning loop #4: teaching the depth we already built (diegetic, just-in-time onboarding)

Battle #135 shipped real depth (per-phase HUD, E/SPACE/X/F/1/2) but a new captain meets it as a wall of unlabelled keys. Reading: diegetic-tutorial craft (Inscryption's rulebook/bell, Super Meat Boy teaching-by-playing) and just-in-time onboarding (NerdyTeachers; the Mario "first enemy is a Goomba" rule).

Takeaways for Tidewake:

1. **Teach at the moment of relevance, not up front.** Don't dump six keys in a menu. Surface each verb exactly when its phase opens — the per-phase HUD is already the perfect hook; it just needs the key attached to the phase it belongs to. Progressive disclosure beats a control-list every time.
2. **Show, don't tell — make the prompt diegetic.** The teaching should read as the world speaking (a bosun's shout, a glowing gun-lanyard), not a grey tooltip. The juice *is* the instruction.
3. **First fight = a Goomba.** The debut battle should be a deliberately soft, winnable duel that tests only the key just taught — one skill per layer, mastery before the next.

**Cross-connection (outside games):** aviation *flight directors* and checklists surface the single next action in context, right when the pilot needs it — not a 200-page manual pre-flight. Same principle: legibility is delivered just-in-time, at the point of decision, so expertise feels like guidance rather than memorisation.

Sources: The Diegetic Tutorial (Aethermon); Onboarding Methods (NerdyTeachers); Diegetic Interface (TV Tropes).

### 2026-07-02 — Deep-reading #5: the world REMEMBERS you — turning a closed loop into a "one more voyage" hook

THE RISE closed the action→feedback→progression→mastery loop; the next depth isn't a new verb, it's making the world *remember* what I did with it. Study on roguelite compulsion + emergent narrative.

1. **A remembered rival is the cheapest bespoke story.** The Nemesis System turns ordinary deeds (I fled him / I beat him) into narrative: a named captain who escalates if he wins (bigger ship next voyage) or nurses a grudge if he escaped — and the Ballad already narrates it (Filament/Wireframe on Nemesis; Kasavin on Hades death-as-story-engine). Upgrade the shipped bounty board (#173) into ONE persistent named rival. Maps to the parked **named-persons #142** frontier; huge felt payoff for a tiny state blob.
2. **Never close the tab empty-handed.** "One more run" lives in the gap between reset difficulty and banked power — even a LOST voyage should bank something visible (a scrap of Infamy, a rumour, a plank on the home port) and the Ballad records the defeat as a *chapter* (Bugnet meta-progression; GameRant "respect your time"). Guards the shipped loss-sting (#164) from feeling purely punitive.
3. **Meta-progression must not trivialise the skill core** — buy-a-bigger-ship (#171) should unlock *tougher* bounties/regions in step (region-fixed difficulty #167 already exists) so the man-o'-war still has to fight (Hades-II over-upgrade critique). A tuning guardrail, not new tech.

**Cross-connection (fiction↔non-fiction):** real pirate crews were *democracies* — signed "articles of agreement" splitting plunder by exact shares, even disability comp (Roberts' code: 800 pieces of eight for a lost limb). A "the crew votes the articles" payout beat after a win — stingy shares breed mutiny rumours (feed the Ballad), generous shares buy loyalty — is grounded history AND Monkey-Island comedy. (Generalist Academy; Wayne Savage on Roberts' articles.)

Sources: Nemesis System (Film Stories / Eckstein-Medium); Hades narrative (GameDeveloper/Kasavin, Aversa); roguelite meta-progression (Bugnet, GameRant); pirate articles (Generalist Academy, waynesavage.com).
