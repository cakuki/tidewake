# Retrospective — Loops 4–6 (Retro 2)

_Date: 2026-06-27 · Facilitator: Project Manager · Present: all roles_

Second studio retro, the every-3-4-cycles ritual, run as a subagent. Three cycles since
Retro 1 turned the sailing toy into something with a first verb: you can sail to a **named**
port and **arrive**, and your voyage now survives a refresh. Blameless: improve the system,
not the person. Honest look at **both the game and our own process**.

## What we shipped
- **Loop 4 — Dockable ports + harbourmaster toast (#12, #22).** Three named ports
  (Saltpurse Quay, Barnacle Bottom, Gullet's Rest); sailing into one fires an arrival toast.
  **The first gameplay verb** — there is finally somewhere to go and a beat of acknowledgement
  when you get there. (39 unit tests.)
- **Loop 5 — Save/load voyage to localStorage (#11), TDD.** Position/heading persist across
  reloads; `N` starts a new voyage. Progress stops evaporating. (50 unit tests.)
- **Loop 6 — Modularised `main.js` (#24).** 244 → 138 lines, split into
  `input` / `hud` / `sailing` / `persistence` systems. Behaviour identical; the wiring hotspot
  Retro 1 flagged is now retired.
- **Process changes (owner-driven, mid-block):** deep-learning research loop every 10 cycles;
  retros run as subagents and review studio **+** process; Tech Lead + Project Manager run a
  continuous-observation pass each cycle; clearer hourly-video recipe; context-optimization
  discipline (one self-contained cycle-runner subagent per slice).
- **Totals:** ~50 unit tests + a green headless playtest gate; ~7 public releases tonight
  (latest `v0.0.20260627004415`). Live at https://cakuki.github.io/tidewake/.

## Product / game review
The north-star fantasy is **becoming playable**: sail → find a *named* port → arrive → (soon)
have it matter. That arc is the spine of "rise from one boat to pirate or governor," and we now
have its first vertebra. Persistence means a session can build on itself. Good momentum.

But honestly: **arriving still does nothing.** The verb has no *reward* — no economy, no payoff,
no reason to choose one port over another. We've built the door and there's no room behind it.
The rest of the gaps compound that:

- **No economy / reward for docking.** The single highest-leverage gap. A port you can't *do*
  anything at is a loading screen with a nice view.
- **Art is still primitive.** Boxy ship and islands (#13). Fine for feel, but it caps the
  "charm" half of "realistic world, funny people."
- **No NPCs / other ships.** The sea is beautiful and empty. No rivals, no life, nothing to
  react to or against.
- **No combat, no town/governance.** The two branch destinations of the fantasy aren't seeded.
- **Audio is ambience-only — no music yet.** The **Musician hasn't shipped a single cue.** A
  whole role with a heart-of-the-studio mandate is dark. The sea has no theme.

**Highest-leverage next step:** a **simple port economy** (coins + buy-low/sell-high cargo,
#26). It is the smallest change that converts "arrive" into "earn," gives the player a *goal
they can state* ("get rich enough to buy a real ship / win a town"), and creates the first real
**choice** (which route is profitable?). It also unlocks every downstream system — combat,
crew, governance all need a coin to spend. Pair it with **activating the Musician** (#27) so the
fantasy finally has a *feel*, not just a mechanic.

Where could the result be more **creative/charming** next block? The economy is a chance for
character: harbourmaster banter, ports with personalities (Gullet's Rest gouges you; Barnacle
Bottom is honest and broke), comedic price events ("a glut of salt cod"). And a sailing theme
would do more for *charm-per-byte* than any visual slice. We've been optimising mechanics; the
next block should deliberately optimise **feel**.

## Studio / process review

**Workflow & flow.**
- **What helped:** the one-self-contained-cycle-runner-subagent pattern is *fast* — plan →
  TDD → playtest → release → board, end to end, lean on the orchestrator. Three clean cycles,
  zero red `main`, ~7 releases. The continuous-observation pass (TL/PM) and retiring the
  `main.js` hotspot (#24) both landed as promised.
- **What dulled creativity:** that same single-runner pattern is **fast but lonely.** Each
  slice is one subagent reasoning alone — there's almost no **cross-role creative friction**.
  Game Designer and Musician, our two designated *creative drivers*, **never entered a cycle.**
  We shipped competent engineering and zero authored fun or score. The process is optimising
  throughput at the cost of creative surprise.

**Collaboration & communication.** Hand-offs through `comms/` files held up. But the loop has
**no explicit ideation beat** — DESIGN exists in the runbook yet gets skipped as "technical,"
so slices arrive pre-shaped by whoever runs them. Two creative roles sitting idle while we ship
"features" is a process smell, not a staffing fact.

**Tooling.** Two CI gates + TDD for pure logic keep paying off. **But QA visual checks are
still entirely manual** — the gallery exists (`studio/qa/gallery/`) but holds only a README; no
shot was archived across loops 4-6, so the per-release diff habit from Retro 1 **did not
actually happen.** A habit that isn't in the gate isn't a habit.

**Creativity (process self-review).** The honest finding: **we now have the architecture to be
creative and aren't using it.** `main.js` is modular, so a real **parallel batch** is finally
cheap — yet we kept shipping one serial slice at a time. The next block should prove the
modularisation by running a parallel batch *and* by activating the two creative roles, ideally
together (economy mechanic + sailing theme as one batch on disjoint files).

**Self-improvement (what we'll change):** add a tiny **CREATIVE SPARK** beat to the loop, make
**every slice name a creative driver** (Game Designer or Musician), make the gallery diff
**enforced not aspirational**, and **default to parallel batches** now that the hotspot is gone.

## What worked (keep)
- **Cycle-runner subagent per slice.** Lean, fast, green. Keep it as the execution unit.
- **TDD for pure logic.** Save/load and port-arrival logic specified by failing tests first —
  50 tests, confident refactors.
- **Retiring the hotspot, not scheduling around it (#24).** Exactly the Retro 1 lesson, done.
- **First verb + persistence in three loops.** Real product progress toward the fantasy.
- **Continuous TL/PM observation.** `main` stayed green; no thrash.

## What didn't (improve)
- **The two creative roles never ran.** Game Designer + Musician shipped nothing in 3 loops.
  Our biggest *creative* gap is a **process** gap, not a backlog gap.
- **Per-release gallery diff didn't happen.** Retro 1 action item, unfulfilled — gallery is
  empty. Aspirational habits decay; this one needs teeth.
- **No parallel batch despite now being cheap.** Modularisation's payoff went uncollected.
- **No ideation/design beat.** Slices arrive pre-shaped; no room for a surprising idea to enter.
- **Reward gap in the product.** Arriving at a port still has no payoff (carried into #26).

## Experiments to try
- **If** every cycle opens with a 2-3 line **CREATIVE SPARK** (Game Designer or Musician names
  one charm/fun/feel beat for the slice), **then** shipped slices carry authored character, not
  just mechanics (measure: does each release have one in-tone detail a player would smile at?).
- **If** we run the next block as a **parallel batch** (economy #26 + sailing theme #27, disjoint
  files), **then** modularisation pays off with zero shared-file conflicts (measure: conflicts
  per batch; slices-per-cycle).
- **If** the cycle-runner **fails the cycle** when no gallery shot was archived for a visible
  change, **then** the visual-diff habit actually happens (measure: gallery shots per release).
- **If** the Musician ships one adaptive-ready sailing theme, **then** QA's charm-rubric and a
  fresh player both report the sea "has a feel," not just "nice water."
