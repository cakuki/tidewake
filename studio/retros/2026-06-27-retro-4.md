process**, now aimed at the next question a finished spine raises — **depth or breadth?** — and at
better, **more creative** results.

## Product / game review

**The spine is done and it's fun.** A fresh player can sail to a named port, trade for profit,
fight an NPC with wit, watch their legend climb *along a pole they chose*, and eventually be
*crowned*. Retros 1–3 each named a missing piece (a verb, a reward, reactivity); Retro 4 is the
first where the honest answer is **"the loop is complete."** The two-pole split (#45) is the
keystone — it turns a single rising number into an *identity the player authors*. That's the whole
game in one mechanic.

**So the question changes: DEPTH or BREADTH?** With a complete spine, more polish on the spine has
diminishing returns and more *thin breadth* (another port, another good) adds nouns without drama.
The studio's read:

- **The single most valuable thing is not new content — it's tuning the arc so players reach it.**
  The legend threshold (~12,800) is a long grind, and Tidewake is a **web game** (median session
  ~4.45 min; the first session must deliver a *visible win*, per PM research). A complete arc that
  almost no one reaches in a sitting is a spine players never feel. **Tune the renown curve
  (#57)** — fast early ranks, a stretched-but-reachable legend — is the highest-leverage, cheapest,
  most *shareable* slice on the board. It makes everything we already built pay off. (Balance/
  tuning now has an owner — see process.)
- **Then add DEPTH that complements what exists, not breadth that dilutes it.** Two depth beats
  stand out: **weather & day–night (#58)** — the biggest charm-per-pixel, every screenshot/clip
  gets more shareable, procedural and asset-free — and **real ship-vs-ship cannon combat (#59)** to
  complement (not replace) the Insult Broadside duel, so a fight is a genuine choice: talk them
  down or open the gun ports. Cannon pulls the deep-learning wildcards forward (#35 cannon SFX, and
  #32 glTF hull for the ship that's now firing).
- **One breadth/onboarding beat is worth it: invisible onboarding (#60).** A seeded first-session
  goal + a juicy first-win moment, no tutorial wall — the cheapest retention lever now the arc
  exists. Pairs with the owner's camera-astern request (#49).

**Recommendation: tune first, then deepen.** Bias the next block toward **#57 (tune the curve)**
so the finished arc is actually *felt* in a session, then **depth that makes captures shareable**
(weather #58) and **a real combat choice** (cannon #59). Keep breadth (more ports/goods) low — the
spine doesn't need more nouns, it needs to be *reachable, atmospheric, and dramatic*.

**Note on the deep-learning backlog.** #45/#46/#33/#16 this block all came (directly or in seed)
from the cycle-10 research loop. We *are* mining it — keep going. Deep-learning loop **#2 is due**
(~10 cycles since #1); run it before the well runs dry.

## Studio / process review

**What's reliable now (keep).**
- **Cycle-runner subagents are fast and dependable** — ~20 releases, 182 tests, gate green
  throughout, no escaped prod bugs this block.
- **QA-found bugs were caught and fixed well** — #47's real root cause (OS key-repeat from a held
  throttle, not the save layer) was *diagnosed*, not patched-over. Good debugging discipline.
- **The shared-contract rule (Retro 3, #34) held** — no integration-seam bug repeated this block.
- **PM Desk (#44) works** — the owner filed 8 issues through it the moment it shipped; the
  feedback→triage pipeline is real.

**What still hurts.**
1. **The orchestrator does a lot of manual visual QA per cycle.** Capturing the Chrome-MCP gallery
   shot + eyeballing the diff is happening *in the orchestrator's context* every cycle — exactly
   the heavy, context-burning work the runbook says to delegate. The **cycle-runner's QA step
   should OWN the gallery capture + diff**, returning a verdict in its 5-line summary, so the
   orchestrator stays lean. And the diff is *still eyeball-only* — **#37 (tolerance-based
   deterministic visual diff)** has been open since cycle 10 and would turn the human pass into a
   real automated gate. It keeps losing to feature slices; schedule it.
2. **Balance/tuning had no owner — so it never happened.** We shipped a legend threshold of
   ~12,800 and never tuned it against a real session length. Numbers that shape *fun* (rank
   curves, reward rates, price spreads) drifted with no one accountable for them. **Assign the
   Game Designer as the standing balance/tuning owner**, with a per-block "is the curve fun in a
   real session?" pass — starting with #57.
3. **from-owner P1 bugs risk sitting behind feature slices.** The owner filed P1 *bugs* (#50
   compass drift, #51 swell submerging ports/docks — these break believability in every
   screenshot) through the new PM Desk. They must not queue behind shiny features: **from-owner
   P1 bugs jump the queue** — they're cheap, they fix what's visibly broken, and they make every
   shareable capture clean.

**Creativity (process self-review).** This was a *structurally* creative block — the two-pole
keystone and the crowning are the most ambitious design we've shipped, and they came from mining
the research backlog well. The creative frontier now isn't "add a system" — it's **atmosphere and
felt drama**: weather that tells a story, combat with weight, a curve that *rewards*. The process
risk is the opposite of Retro 2's (creative roles dark): now we could over-build systems and
under-tune *feel*. The balance-owner change is the guard against that.

## What worked (keep)
- **The two-pole keystone (#45) + crowning (#46)** — completed the north-star arc; identity the
  player authors, not a single bar.
- **Real root-cause debugging** (#47 key-repeat) instead of symptom-patching.
- **Cycle-runner reliability + the shared-contract rule holding** — fast, green, no seam bugs.
- **PM Desk owner-feedback intake (#44)** — used immediately; keep it.
- **Mining the deep-learning backlog** — most of this block traces to cycle-10 research.

## What didn't (improve)
- **Visual QA lives in the orchestrator, not the cycle-runner** — burns the context the runbook
  says to protect; and the gallery diff is still eyeball-only (#37 unbuilt since cycle 10).
- **No balance/tuning owner** — the finished arc shipped with an un-tuned ~12,800 grind that a web
  session can't reach.
- **from-owner P1 bugs have no fast lane** — visible-breakage bugs (#50, #51) could queue behind
  features.
- **We risk depth-without-tuning** — building more systems on a spine players can't yet reach in a
  session would compound the grind problem.

  session?" check) starting with #57, **then** the finished arc becomes *reachable* and feel stops

  is fixed before it taxes every screenshot (measure: open from-owner P1 bug age).
- **If** the next block **tunes the curve (#57) before adding depth (#58/#59)**, **then** players
  actually *feel* the complete arc, and depth lands on a spine that pays off (measure: does a
  playtest reach a crowning/near-pole in a focused sitting?).
