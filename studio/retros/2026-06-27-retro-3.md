## Product / game review
**The core fantasy is now legible.** A fresh player can sail to a *named* port, **trade for a
profit**, watch coins climb, and see those coins convert into a **renown rank the game names back
to them** — with NPC ships making the sea feel inhabited rather than staged. That is the spine of
"rise from one boat to feared pirate or beloved governor," and after five cycles it's a *stateable
goal a player can pursue in one short session*, not a tech demo. Retro 2's two big bets — activate
the creative roles, default to parallel — both paid off: the Musician shipped a theme and two of
five cycles were clean parallel batches.

**What's still missing is the part that makes a story.** Renown climbs, but **the world doesn't
yet react to it** — the rank is a number on the player, not a change in how ports and ships treat
them. NPC ships wander but you can't *do* anything to or with them. And the hull is still a box.
The honest read: we have a competent economic loop and the *seed* of identity, but no **drama**
and not yet much **charm-per-pixel**.

**Next highest-leverage fun beat — ranked:**
1. **Port reputation that reacts to you (#39-followup, seed of #33/#4).** The cheapest way to
   convert renown from a number into a *living world*: ports greet you by rank, prices soften or
   harden, a harbourmaster "remembers" you, lean-pirate ranks make merchant sloops sheer away and
   a navy take interest. One mechanic dramatizes the whole north-star and is the PM's own
   deep-learning wildcard ("The World Knows Your Name"). Comedy rides free: NPC reactions get
   wittier as your legend grows.
2. **The CC0 glTF ship (#32).** The single biggest **charm-per-byte** upgrade. Every screenshot,
   every clip, the whole first impression improves the moment the box becomes a real hull. Slots
   cleanly behind the existing ship seam; low risk, high delight. Pair its swap with a gallery
   shot so the visual win is recorded.
3. **"Insult Broadside" comedic combat (#33).** The marquee *authored-fun* beat and the purest
   expression of "realistic world, funny people." It needs the most design and a morale meter, so
   it's the right *next big* slice but not the next *quick* one — seed it after reputation gives
   NPC encounters stakes to fight over.
4. **Minimap / discovery (#16).** Real quality-of-life — makes the trade loop legible (where are
   the ports? which route?) — but it's utility, not a fun *beat*. Schedule it as the "B-side" of a
   parallel batch, not a headline.

Keep the realism + comedy balance: reputation reactions and Insult Broadside are where the comedy
lives; the glTF hull and minimap keep the world believable.

## Studio / process review

**Workflow & flow — what helped.**
- **Parallel batches finally landed (#26+#27, #28+#31).** Two clean two-slice batches proved the
  #24 modularisation. The creative-driver mandate produced the first real music *in the same batch*
  — Retro 2's headline bets both converted.
- **The deep-learning loop was a creativity multiplier.** Nine agents in isolated contexts
  produced nine genuinely buildable, on-tone ideas (#32–#40) — including the two best things on the
  board right now (reputation reactions, Insult Broadside). Real serendipity, real craft.
- **TDD kept scaling cleanly** — 73 → 114 tests across the block; the renown ladder was specified
  by failing tests first.

**Workflow & flow — what hurt.**
- **The integration-seam bug (#29) was predictable, and we predicted it — then shipped it anyway.**
  Two parallel slices (#26 economy, #27 theme — and really the trade panel/state contract) disagreed
  on a `state.port` getter + buy-by-name shape. The PM's *own* deep-learning takeaway says: **no
  parallel dispatch across a shared state/data seam without a contract artifact both sides assert
  (#34)**. We had the lesson written down the same night and didn't yet have it in the runbook, so
  the batch went out without the contract step. The fix is one cycle of rework we can stop paying.
- **The recurring transient subagent glitch.** A few cycle-runner / research subagents returned
  **0 tool uses** (came back empty / did nothing) and had to be **re-dispatched**. It's transient
  and re-dispatch fixes it, but it's silent — if the orchestrator doesn't notice the empty return,
  a cycle stalls. This needs a named, standard response, not ad-hoc rescue.

**Tooling.**
- **QA autopilot is fiddly across a coordinate mismatch.** Driving the ship to a port to test
  docking/trade tripped on **`port.pos` being `[x, z]` (2D, ground plane) while ship state is
  `[x, y, z]` (3D)**. Easy to index the wrong axis and sail to nowhere; QA burned time on it.
  There's no shared helper, so every QA pass re-derives the navigation math by hand.
- **The QA real-time-vs-synchronous-step lesson cost us a false bug (#30).** Synchronous
  `tw.step()` advances the *sim* but **not wall-clock**, so CSS fade-in transitions (`#trade .show`
  opacity) read mid-flight and QA flagged a "bug" that was a transition timing artifact. Confirmed
  by the deep-learning research the same night (`step()` never advances CSS animations — wait on
  `transitionend` or disable animations for baselines). Lesson is captured in QA memory; it should
  also be a reusable helper so no one re-learns it.
- **Gallery diff is happening but still eyeball-only.** Shots were archived this block (economy,
  trade, NPC) — the Retro-2 teeth held. #37 (tolerance-based deterministic diff) would make it a
  real automated gate.

**Creativity (process self-review).** The block was our **most creative yet** — first music, first
research loop, NPCs, a named-identity ladder. The creative-driver beat is no longer aspirational.
The remaining creative gap is **reactivity**: we keep adding *nouns* (ports, goods, ships, ranks)
and not enough *verbs that make the world respond*. The next block should bias toward **the world
reacting** (reputation, NPC behaviour) over more inert content.

**Self-improvement (what we'll change).** Adopt the **#34 shared-contract step into the runbook
now** — it's a written lesson that already cost us a bug. Add a **re-dispatch rule for glitched
(0-tool-use) subagents**. Add a **QA navigation/coordinate helper** (and the real-time-wait note)
so the `[x,z]`/`[x,y,z]` and `step()`-vs-wall-clock traps are solved once. Bias the next block
toward reactive verbs.

## What worked (keep)
- **Parallel batches with creative drivers** — #26+#27 shipped mechanics *and* feel together.
- **The deep-learning research loop** — highest creative ROI of any single cycle; keep the
  every-10 cadence.
- **TDD for pure logic** — 114 tests; the renown ladder and economy specified test-first.
- **QA catching the seam bug pre-release (#29)** and honestly retiring a non-bug (#30) instead of
  "fixing" a phantom — the two-axis gate working as designed.
- **Enforced gallery shots actually happened this block** — Retro 2's teeth held.

## What didn't (improve)
- **Known lesson, unadopted, repeated.** The shared-contract gap (#34) was in PM research the same
  night #29 bit us. Lessons must reach the **runbook** to count — research that isn't operationalised
  is just reading.
- **Silent empty-subagent returns** stalled cycles until noticed; no standard response existed.
- **QA coordinate/timing traps** (`[x,z]` vs `[x,y,z]`; `step()` vs wall-clock) cost real time and
  one false bug — solvable once with a helper, not re-learned each pass.
- **We're adding nouns faster than reactions.** The world still doesn't respond to who the player
  is becoming — the renown number has no consequences yet.

## Experiments to try
- **If** the PM posts a one-line **shared-contract artifact** (name · shape · owner · consumers)
  before any parallel batch that crosses a state/save/event seam, **then** integration-seam bugs
  like #29 drop toward zero (measure: seam bugs per parallel batch).
- **If** the orchestrator treats a **0-tool-use subagent return as an automatic re-dispatch**
  (once), **then** the transient glitch never silently stalls a cycle (measure: stalled cycles).
- **If** QA uses a shared **navigation helper** (`sailToPort` that reconciles `port.pos [x,z]` →
  ship `[x,y,z]`, plus a real-time settle wait), **then** autopilot tests stop mis-indexing axes
  and stop false-flagging fade-in timing (measure: QA setup time; false bugs).
- **If** the next block makes **ports/NPCs react to renown** before adding new content, **then** a
  fresh player reports the world "knows who I am," not just "I can trade" (measure: does a playtest
  produce a *story*, not just a transaction?).
